### Detailed Stakeholder Profiles

#### 1. Core Team (High Power, High Interest)

**Who**: Release manager, engineering lead, community lead, security lead (4-5 people)

**Interest**: Successful release execution, career growth, project sustainability

**Concerns**:
- Meeting launch deadlines
- Quality standards maintained
- Team workload manageable
- Technical debt not accumulated

**Communication Needs**:
- **Frequency**: Daily during beta/launch, weekly post-launch
- **Channel**: Slack #releases, daily stand-ups, email
- **Content**: Status updates, blockers, decisions needed
- **Tone**: Collaborative, technical, action-oriented

**Engagement Strategy**:
- Include in all major decisions (release gates, go/no-go)
- Provide autonomy within their domain
- Recognize contributions publicly
- Address concerns promptly

---

#### 2. Beta Testers (Medium Power, High Interest)

**Interest**: Early access, influence product direction, recognition

## Communication Strategy

### Communication Principles

**1. Transparency**
- Share both successes and challenges openly
- Admit mistakes, explain learnings
- No hidden agendas or surprise announcements

**2. Consistency**
- Same message across all channels (no contradictions)
- Regular cadence (weekly, monthly, quarterly)
- Predictable timing (e.g., Friday 4 PM for weekly updates)

**3. Timeliness**
- Communicate early (don't wait until perfect)
- Respond quickly to questions (within 24-48 hours)
- Update stakeholders on progress (don't go dark)

**4. Relevance**
- Tailor content to stakeholder interests
- Avoid information overload (respect attention)
- Signal-to-noise ratio: High value, low volume

**5. Two-Way**
- Listen actively to stakeholder feedback
- Incorporate feedback into decisions
- Close the feedback loop (tell them what you did)

## Communication Channels

### Channel Strategy

**Principles**:
1. **Right channel for right audience**: Discord for users, Email for sponsors
2. **One source of truth**: GitHub is canonical for technical decisions
3. **Cross-promote**: Announce on one channel, link to details elsewhere
4. **Low friction**: Easy to subscribe/unsubscribe, no spam

---

### Channel Inventory

#### 1. Discord (Primary Community Hub)

**Channels**:
- `#announcements` (read-only): Major releases, important updates
- `#general`: Casual discussion, introductions
- `#help`: User support questions
- `#contributors`: Development discussion
- `#beta-testing`: Beta tester coordination (temporary)

**Audience**: Core team, contributors, active users, community (500+ members)

**Frequency**:
- Announcements: Weekly during active development, bi-weekly otherwise
- #help: Monitored daily, responses within 4 hours (P2 SLA)

**Tone**: Friendly, helpful, inclusive

**Best Practices**:
- Use @everyone sparingly (major announcements only)
- Pin important messages
- Use threads for discussions
- React with emojis to acknowledge

---

#### 2. Twitter (@caro_cli)

**Audience**: General public, potential users, tech community (1,000+ followers)

**Frequency**: 1-2 posts/day during launch week, 3-5/week otherwise

**Content Types**:
- Release announcements
- Feature highlights and tips
- Community highlights (user stories, contributor spotlights)
- Retweets of user mentions

**Tone**: Concise, energetic, visual (include screenshots/GIFs)

**Best Practices**:
- Use hashtags (#CLI #commandline #opensource)
- Tag users when featuring their content
- Respond to mentions within 24 hours
- Use Twitter threads for longer announcements

---

#### 3. GitHub (Technical Hub)

**Locations**:
- **Releases**: Official release notes with full changelog
- **Issues**: Bug reports, feature requests, discussions
- **Discussions**: General Q&A, ideas, polls
- **README**: Project overview, quick start

**Audience**: Developers, contributors, power users (1,000+ stars)

**Frequency**:
- Releases: Per release (v1.1.0-beta, v1.1.1, etc.)
- Issues: Monitored daily, triaged within 24 hours
- Discussions: Monitored 2-3x/week

**Tone**: Technical, precise, professional

**Best Practices**:
- Use labels consistently (bug, feature, P0-P3)
- Close issues with reference to fixing PR
- Pin important issues/discussions
- Keep README up to date

---

#### 4. Email

**Lists**:
- **Beta testers list** (5 subscribers): Daily during beta testing
- **Sponsors list** (0 subscribers, future): Quarterly reports
- **Press list** (0 subscribers, future): Major announcements

**Frequency**: Varies by list (see above)

**Tone**: Varies by audience (appreciative for beta, professional for sponsors)

**Best Practices**:
- Personalize where possible (use recipient name)
- Clear subject lines (e.g., "Caro v1.1.0-beta Beta Testing Begins")
- Include unsubscribe link
- Mobile-friendly formatting

---

#### 5. Blog (Dev.to + Website)

**Audience**: Developers, potential users, community (via Dev.to reach)

**Frequency**: 2-3 posts/week during launch, 1-2/week otherwise

**Content Types**:
- Tutorials ("How to find large files with Caro")
- Deep dives ("How Caro's safety validation works")
- Release announcements (long-form)
- Community spotlights

**Tone**: Educational, informative, example-heavy

**Best Practices**:
- Cross-post between Dev.to and website
- Include code examples and screenshots
- Link to relevant docs
- Engage with comments

---

#### 6. Reddit (r/commandline)

**Audience**: CLI enthusiasts, Unix/Linux users (50k+ subreddit members)

**Frequency**: Weekly during launch, bi-weekly otherwise

**Content Types**:
- Release announcements
- Tutorials and tips
- "Show and tell" demonstrations
- Responses to Caro mentions

**Tone**: Humble, authentic, helpful (Reddit values authenticity)

**Best Practices**:
- Don't over-promote (follow 90/10 rule: 90% helpful, 10% promotional)
- Respond to comments promptly
- Be honest about limitations
- Respect subreddit rules (no spam)

## Escalation Procedures

### When to Escalate

**Escalate to Release Manager** if:
- P0 or P1 bug discovered
- Release gate blocked
- Stakeholder concern unresolved
- Timeline at risk

**Escalate to Project Lead** if:
- Release must be delayed or cancelled
- Major strategic decision needed (e.g., change scope)
- Conflict between stakeholders
- Legal or compliance issue

---

### Escalation Process

**Step 1: Identify Issue** (immediately)
- What is the problem?
- Who is affected?
- What is the impact?
- What is the urgency?

**Step 2: Attempt Resolution** (within SLA)
- Can this be resolved without escalation?
- What resources are needed?
- Who can help?

**Step 3: Escalate** (if unresolved)
- Notify escalation point (Slack, email, call if urgent)
- Provide context (issue, impact, attempts to resolve)
- Request decision or resources

**Step 4: Follow Up** (after resolution)
- Communicate resolution to affected stakeholders
- Document lesson learned
- Update procedures if needed

---

### Escalation Matrix

| Issue Type | Severity | Escalate To | Response Time |
|------------|----------|-------------|---------------|
| P0 bug (release blocker) | Critical | Eng Lead, Rel Manager | 1 hour |
| Security vulnerability | Critical | Sec Lead, Eng Lead | 1 hour |
| Release delay needed | High | Project Lead | 4 hours |
| Stakeholder conflict | Medium | Rel Manager, Proj Lead | 24 hours |
| Communication issue | Low | Comm Lead | 48 hours |

**Step 4: Respond** (within SLA):
- Acknowledge feedback within 24 hours
- Provide timeline for resolution (if applicable)
- Thank user for feedback

**Step 5: Close Loop** (after action):
- Notify user when issue resolved
- Ask if resolution satisfactory
- Incorporate learning into process

---

### Feedback Analysis

**Weekly** (30 minutes):
- Review feedback trends (common themes?)
- Quantify (how many users reporting X?)
- Share insights with team

**Example**:
> Week 1 feedback summary:
> - 23 support questions (most common: "How do I uninstall?")
> - 8 bug reports (5 P3, 2 P2, 1 P1)
> - 12 feature requests (top: shell history integration)
> - 5 testimonials/praise
> - 0 complaints about privacy or safety
>
